## A. Dashboard (hero) — `darwin-A-quicklog/step-01-baseline.png`

**[Hero · the $2,045.59 number is the right hero, and it correctly dominates] → keep.** This is the one decision-driving number ("safe to spend") and it is the largest, boldest element. Typographic hierarchy here is correct. **(no action — strength)**

**[Hero math sub-line · `$4,800 − $2,154 bills − $600 living = $2,046` rendered in mixed orange/red/pink monospace] → HIGH.** The breakdown under the hero uses a typewriter-style monospace with colour-coded terms (red `$2,154`, orange `$600`/`$4,800`). On a dark hero card this reads as "code / debug output," not a premium money summary. Red on a deduction is fine, but the all-monospace, multi-colour line undercuts the polish of the hero above it. Recommendation: set the breakdown in the app's normal sans, use a single muted secondary colour for the equation, reserve red only for the genuinely-negative/at-risk term, and right-align the result. Why it matters: the sub-line is the *justification* for the hero — it must feel as trustworthy as the number it explains. `step-01-baseline.png`

**[Hero · "Liquid net worth · tap for full picture" + `+$23,625.25`] → MED.** The "tap for full picture" affordance is in very faint grey (`--text3`-class) against the dark card — on an S23 in daylight this is near-invisible. Per slyght's own contrast rule, the tappable hint should be at least secondary-contrast. Why it matters: if the affordance can't be seen, the tap-for-detail pattern is dead. `step-01-baseline.png`

**[Hero · "Running $60.00 under pace this week ✅" inside the MAX PER DAY card] → MED.** This reassurance line is small and low-contrast grey-green. The MAX-PER-DAY card's hero number `$60.00` is good, but the supporting status text is the part John reads daily for emotional state ("am I okay?") and it recedes too far. Bump to secondary contrast. `step-01-baseline.png`

**[Bottom band · "IMMEDIATE DEBTS $775 total" with an "Auto-sort ↕" control] → LOW.** "Auto-sort" is mild jargon and the control glyph is small. Confirm it meets 44×44px and consider "Sort" with a clearer label. `step-01-baseline.png`

## B. Quick Log modal — Savings path (THE BROKEN FLOW)
`darwin-A-quicklog/step-02..06`

**[step-03 · Type = "Savings" selected, but NO destination/goal picker appears] → HIGH (top finding).** When the user switches Type from Expense to Savings, the Category row simply *disappears* and nothing replaces it — there is no "which goal?" selector. The modal looks almost identical to an expense log. The user has three real savings goals (Darwin Trip, China Holiday, Rainy Day Fund — proven to exist in flow B) and the UI gives them no way to pick one. This actively *misleads*: the green "Log It" button implies a complete, correct action. **Recommendation:** when Type=Savings, reveal a required goal-picker (reuse the goal chips/cards from the Savings-plan sub-screen) before "Log It" is enabled; if no goal is chosen, the save must not silently succeed. Why it matters: in a money app, a save that goes *nowhere* is worse than an error — the user believes their $300 is earmarked when it isn't. `step-03-select-savings.png`

**[step-05 · post-submit toast reads "−$300.00 · Savings · — · Tap to undo"] → HIGH.** The literal em-dash "—" sits where the destination goal name should be — visible proof the money landed in no bucket. A premium app would say "$300 → Darwin Trip." The dash is a tell that the data model recorded an orphaned allocation. **Recommendation:** the toast must name the destination; if there is no destination, that's the bug above surfacing — block the save. `step-05-submit.png`

**[step-04→05→06 · hero drops $2,045.59 → $1,914.85 → $1,782.50 across consecutive frames for a single $300 log] → HIGH.** The hero is not settling on a stable value: it shows ~$130 drop at submit, then continues dropping to a different value in aftermath, for one $300 entry. Whatever the cause (re-render race, double-apply, or savings being double-counted against "safe to spend"), the headline number visibly *moves on its own*. Nothing destroys trust in a money app faster than a balance that changes when you're just looking at it. **Recommendation:** investigate the write/render path; the hero must reflect one deterministic value after a single committed log. (Likely overlaps known FR / balance-apply work — flag to engineering, not a pure UX fix.) `step-04-enter-300.png` · `step-05-submit.png` · `step-06-aftermath.png`

**[step-02 · Category chips row scrolls horizontally with a third chip ("Sho…" = Shopping) clipped at the right edge] → MED.** The cut-off chip is a discoverability trap — users may not realise the row scrolls. Add a fade/peek affordance or a chevron, or wrap to two rows. Same applies to the Type row where a 4th option ("T…" likely Transfer) is clipped. `step-02-open-quicklog.png`

**[step-02 · "Quick-log a spend from your Virgin Money notification" subtitle] → LOW.** Good plain-English microcopy and the real bank name is a nice trust signal. But it says "a spend" while the modal also handles Savings/Income — slightly narrowing. Consider "Quick-log money in or out." `step-02-open-quicklog.png`

**[step-02 · "Scan a document — let AI fill this in" dashed CTA] → LOW.** Strong, well-placed. Keep. (Strength.)

## D. Payday Plan — Savings sub-screen (the WORKING allocation surface)
`darwin-B-plantick/step-03, step-06`

**[step-03 · goal cards with progress bars, "$800 of $4,000 goal (20%)", per-goal "$0 this cycle"] → strength.** This is the premium surface — clear hierarchy, lifetime-vs-this-cycle distinction spelled out in plain English ("The amount on the right is what you're putting in *this cycle only*"). **[step-03 · "POOL TO ALLOCATE — $542 to split across goals" with monospace breakdown `$1,141 surplus − $298 provisions − $300 safety buffer = $542`] → MED.** Same monospace/code-look critique as the hero sub-line (A). ## E. Payday Plan canvas — lock / unlock loop (NO VISIBLE FEEDBACK)
`plan-lock/step-01..06` and `darwin-B-plantick/step-01..02`

## F. Bills / Calendar — mark paid + undo
`bills-mark-paid/step-01..05`

**[step-01 · calendar is genuinely premium] → strength.** Clean month grid, sensible legend (Bill / Payday / Bill+debt), today (26) ringed, payday (15) highlighted green. This is the most polished surface in the batch. Keep. **(strength.)**

**[step-01..05 · per-day bill amounts ($259, $50, $15, $30, $125) in faint grey on pale-pink cells] → HIGH (contrast).** The dollar amounts — the actual information on the calendar — are low-contrast grey on pink and hard to read at a glance, especially the smaller ones. The day numbers are darker than the amounts, inverting the priority (the amount is what John scans for). **Recommendation:** darken bill amounts to readable contrast; they're the payload. `bills-mark-paid/step-01-baseline.png`

**[step-03→04→05 · "after-paid", "undo", and "after-undo" frames are all identical] → HIGH.** The undo action produced no visible change: the toast still reads "Phone Plan paid — $50 logged" and NW stays $23,575.25 across all three frames. Either there was no undo control to tap, or undo ran without re-rendering / without reverting the balance. For a money app, an undo that gives no feedback (or doesn't work) is a trust-breaker — the user can't tell if their correction took. **Recommendation:** confirm undo actually reverts state, and show explicit "Undone — Phone Plan moved back to due" feedback. (Likely the writer-key vs reader-key issue named in the flow title — flag to engineering.) `bills-mark-paid/step-04-undo.png` · `step-05-after-undo.png`

**[step-02 · "Living · $60.00/day × 6 days = $360" (baseline) becomes "Living · $10.00/day × 6 days = $60" after paying a bill] → MED.** Paying a $50 phone bill should not change the *living per-day rate* from $60 to $10. **[step-01 · "Running $60.00 under pace — good week" (green) vs after-paid "Running $40.00 over pace — slow down" (amber)] → LOW.** The pace banner colour-shift (green→amber) and tone ("good week"→"slow down") is good coaching UX. ## Cross-cutting patterns (apply app-wide)

1. **Monospace + multi-colour math lines read as debug output.** Seen on the dashboard hero sub-line (A), the Savings pool breakdown (D), and the canvas "WHERE THE $5,000 SITS NOW" line (E). Replace the typewriter font + rainbow colour-coding with the app's sans, one muted secondary colour, red reserved strictly for genuinely-negative terms. These lines are the *justification* for the headline numbers and must feel trustworthy. **HIGH cumulative.**

2. **Grey-on-grey / grey-on-pink on information (not decoration).** Calendar bill amounts (F), canvas reconciliation line (E), hero affordance hints (A). slyght's own rule: primary numbers white/high-contrast, labels secondary, faint grey for decoration ONLY. Several *information* elements are currently at decoration contrast. **HIGH cumulative.**

3. **State-changing actions with no visible feedback.** Lock/unlock (E, all 6 identical), override-set (D), bill undo (F). Every money-moving or commitment action must produce an immediate, unmistakable visual state change. This is the trust backbone of a finance app and it's currently missing on three surfaces. **HIGH cumulative.**

4. **Hero/balance numbers are not deterministic across renders.** Both log flows (B, C) showed the hero drifting across consecutive frames for a single entry. Engineering issue, but it's the most trust-corrosive thing in the batch from a *user-perceived* standpoint. **HIGH.**

5. **Horizontally-scrolling chip rows clip their last option with no peek/fade.** Quick-Log Category and Type rows (B). Add a fade/chevron or wrap. **MED.**
